The $ENS token is the governance token of the ENS DAO. Every major decision about the ENS protocol — from fee changes to registrar upgrades to treasury allocation — is decided by $ENS token holders through on-chain voting. Holding $ENS gives you a stake in ENS governance, but you must delegate your tokens before your voting power becomes active.

<Warning>
  The ENS airdrop occurred in **November 2021** for anyone who held an ENS name on October 31, 2021. **There are no plans for another airdrop.** Be skeptical of any announcements claiming a new ENS airdrop — these are very likely fraudulent.
</Warning>

## Token Distribution

At the genesis airdrop in November 2021, the 100 million $ENS supply was allocated across three groups:

| Allocation | Share | Amount |
|------------|-------|--------|
| Past ENS users (airdrop) | 25% | 25,000,000 $ENS |
| Contributors (team, advisors, key contributors) | 25% | 25,000,000 $ENS |
| DAO treasury | 50% | 50,000,000 $ENS |

The DAO treasury's 50 million $ENS is controlled entirely by governance — it can only be spent via passed proposals and is the primary resource for funding ENS development, working groups, and public goods grants.

## Activating Your Voting Power

Holding $ENS is not enough to participate in governance — you must **delegate** your tokens first. Delegation registers your voting power with a specific address. Until you delegate, your $ENS balance has no weight in any vote.

You can delegate to yourself (to vote directly) or to another community member (to let them vote on your behalf). Delegation does not transfer or lock your tokens — you retain full ownership and can redelegate or transfer at any time.

<Note>
  Your voting power for any given proposal is calculated from your delegated $ENS balance at the **snapshot block** — the block number recorded when the proposal was created. Changes to your delegation after that block will not affect your weight on that proposal.
</Note>

### How to Delegate

<Steps>
  <Step title="Go to the ENS App or a governance portal">
    Navigate to [app.ens.domains](https://app.ens.domains) or [app.anticapture.com/ens](https://app.anticapture.com/ens) and connect your wallet.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Find the delegation section">
    On the ENS app, look for the governance or delegation panel. On Anticapture, select "Delegate" from the governance dashboard.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Choose your delegatee">
    Enter the address you want to delegate to. To vote yourself, enter your own wallet address. To delegate to a community member, search for active delegates and review their voting history and stated positions.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Confirm the transaction">
    Submit the delegation transaction. This calls the `delegate(address delegatee)` function on the $ENS token contract. Pay the required gas fee and confirm in your wallet.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Verify your delegation">
    After the transaction confirms, your delegated voting power will appear on the governance portal. You are now ready to vote on active proposals.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Tip>
  If you hold $ENS but don't have time to follow governance closely, delegate to an active community delegate whose values align with yours. You can review delegates' voting records and written rationales on [Anticapture](https://app.anticapture.com/ens) before choosing.
</Tip>

## Recovering Accidentally Sent Tokens

If you accidentally sent $ENS tokens to the wrong address, your recovery options depend on where the tokens went:

- **Sent to `token.ensdao.eth` or `wallet.ensdao.eth`** — Recovery may be possible. Contact the [Meta-Governance working group](https://discuss.ens.domains/c/meta-governance/28) and explain your situation.
- **Sent to the null address (`0x000...0000`) or a mistyped address** — The tokens are permanently unrecoverable. No one can retrieve them.
- **Sent to an exchange or third-party wallet** — Contact that exchange or third party directly for assistance.

<Warning>
  Always double-check the recipient address before sending $ENS. Blockchain transactions are irreversible, and most misdirected transfers cannot be recovered.
</Warning>
