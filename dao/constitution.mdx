The ENS DAO Constitution is the foundational legal and ethical framework for ENS governance. It defines the binding rules that determine which governance actions are legitimate — and which are not. Every proposal passed by the DAO must be consistent with these articles, and no majority vote, however large, can override them except through the formal amendment process described in Article V.

<Note>
  The examples under each article are illustrative only. They help clarify intent but are **not** binding parts of the constitutional text itself.
</Note>

## Article I — Name Ownership Shall Not Be Infringed

ENS governance will not enact any change that infringes on the rights of ENS users to retain names they own, or unfairly discriminates against name owners' ability to extend, transfer, or otherwise use their names.

This article establishes that name ownership is inviolable. Once you register an ENS name, the DAO cannot take it away, discriminate against your ability to renew it, or treat your name differently from others without a transparent, objective basis.

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Permissible example">
    ENS governance **may** enact a change affecting the registration or extension costs of all names based on transparent criteria such as name length, as long as it pursues a goal outlined in the Constitution. Blanket, objective fee changes are acceptable.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Not permissible example">
    ENS governance **must not** enact a change increasing or reducing extension costs for a specific handpicked list of existing ENS names. Targeting individual names or groups of owners would constitute unfair discrimination.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## Article II — Fees Are Primarily an Incentive Mechanism

The primary purpose of registration fees is as an incentive mechanism to prevent the namespace from becoming overwhelmed with speculatively registered names. A secondary purpose is to provide enough revenue to fund the ongoing development and improvement of ENS. ENS governance will not enact any fee other than for these purposes.

This article ensures that registration fees serve the ecosystem rather than functioning as a pure revenue-extraction tool. The DAO can raise fees to address demonstrated squatting or to cover reasonable operational costs — but it cannot impose fees simply to maximize income.

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Permissible example">
    ENS governance **may** increase the price of name registrations to address excessive speculative registrations where the current price is set too low, or because the current price is insufficient to fund ongoing ENS operations at a reasonable level.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Not permissible example">
    ENS governance **must not** enact a change imposing a fee for claiming DNS domains inside ENS, because such a fee would be purely an income-generating measure and not a genuine incentive mechanism.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## Article III — Income Funds ENS and Other Public Goods

Any income generated to the ENS treasury is to be used first of all to ensure the long-term viability of ENS, and to fund the continuing development and improvement of the ENS system. Funds that are not reasonably required to achieve this goal may be used to fund other public goods within web3 as ENS governance sees fit.

ENS governance will not allocate funds to a team or individual who does not commit to upholding the same principles outlined in this Constitution in their use of allocated funds.

This article creates a clear spending hierarchy: ENS development first, web3 public goods second, and nothing that conflicts with ENS's goals. It also makes fund recipients accountable to the same constitutional values.

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Permissible example">
    ENS governance **may** offer grant funding for a public good unrelated to ENS or Ethereum, so long as doing so does not affect the long-term viability of ENS.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Not permissible example">
    ENS governance **must not** use treasury funds to support projects that conflict with the goals of ENS — for example, funding a competing naming system that undermines ENS's mission.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## Article IV — ENS Integrates with the Global Namespace

In order to facilitate making the most widely usable naming system, ENS aims to integrate with the legacy DNS naming system to the greatest extent possible without sacrificing the decentralization of ENS. ENS governance will not enact changes that compromise ENS's ability to do this.

This article reflects ENS's core design principle: ENS exists to complement and interoperate with the global DNS, not to fragment it. The DAO should not allocate top-level domains that conflict with established gTLDs or ccTLDs, since doing so would create confusion and undermine the global usability of ENS names.

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Permissible example">
    ENS governance **should** grant control of a top-level domain to its owner in the DNS system on request — for example, allowing `.com` DNS domain owners to claim their ENS equivalents.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Not permissible example">
    ENS governance **must not** create new top-level domains unless those domains have been granted to ENS by a DNS authority. Inventing new TLDs that conflict with the global DNS namespace is prohibited.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## Article V — Amendments Require a Supermajority

Any change may be made to this Constitution only by a two-thirds majority **and** at least 1% of all tokens participating.

This article sets a deliberately high bar for constitutional change. Ordinary governance proposals require a simple majority (50%) with 1% quorum. Amending the Constitution requires both a two-thirds supermajority of votes cast **and** at least 1% of the total $ENS supply to participate — ensuring that any change to the foundational rules of ENS has broad, meaningful community support.

<Warning>
  Submitting a Constitutional Amendment follows a distinct proposal process. Your draft must include a diff showing the exact changes you propose to make to the Constitution. See the [Proposals](/dao/proposals) page for the full submission process.
</Warning>

| Proposal Type            | Quorum Required | Approval Threshold |
|--------------------------|-----------------|--------------------|
| Executable Proposal      | 1% of supply    | Simple majority (>50%) |
| Social Proposal          | 1% of supply    | Simple majority (>50%) |
| Constitutional Amendment | 1% of supply    | Two-thirds (≥66.7%) |
